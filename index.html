Selected Work

When Your Loyalty Program Lives in Three Places

Omnichannel Loyalty · Global Fashion Retailer · 2024

The retailer had 20 million loyalty members scattered across mobile, web, and in-store systems that didn't talk to each other. Someone could earn points in a store but not see them on their phone. We couldn't just rebuild everything—we had to move millions of people to a new platform without anyone noticing. I mapped every touchpoint where members interacted with the program, interviewed customers about what mattered most, then designed a migration that felt invisible. People kept shopping, points kept working, and nobody called support asking where their account went.

The Screen Wasn't the Problem

Transit Ticketing · ALSA · 2023

ALSA redesigned their ticket machine screens and nothing changed. People still jammed credit cards into the cash slot because a poorly placed LED made it light up at the wrong time. Redesigning the whole machine wasn't realistic, so I focused on what we could control: making the physical affordances align with the digital interface. We repositioned lights, added clearer labels at decision points, and designed the screen flow to work with—not against—how rushed people actually behave. Fewer jammed machines, shorter lines, and staff stopped having to stand next to the kiosks explaining what to do.

When the Metaphor Stops Working

Enterprise Software Redesign · 2023

The software used a "filing cabinet" metaphor—folders, drawers, archives. It made sense in 2005. By 2023, new hires had never used a filing cabinet and couldn't figure out where anything was. I watched people search for documents they'd just created because "filing" them felt like putting them away forever. We didn't just redesign screens—we rebuilt the mental model around how people actually work now: streams, pins, and recent activity. Once the metaphor matched their reality, navigation became obvious. Support tickets about "lost files" dropped to nearly zero.

First Days Shouldn't Feel Like Homework

Employee Onboarding · Zebra Technologies · 2023

New hires at Zebra were quitting within weeks because onboarding felt like drowning in policy documents. HR teams across regions ran it differently, and nobody remembered anything they'd read. I talked to recent hires who described their first month as "lonely" and "overwhelming." We built Zebra Island—a 30-day experience where people learn by exploring, not reading. Information comes up when it's relevant, not all at once. New hires could connect with colleagues naturally while their calendar stayed structured but not suffocating. HR stopped chasing people to complete modules because they actually wanted to.
